# Frequently Asked Questions — Student Mental Health & Productivity

## Study & Focus

### "How do I focus when I can't concentrate?"

Difficulty focusing is usually caused by one of these: fatigue, anxiety, environmental distractions, or tasks that feel too large to start.

**Try these steps:**
1. **Identify the cause first.** Are you tired? Anxious? Distracted by environment?
2. **If tired**: Take a 20-minute nap or do 5 minutes of box breathing to reset.
3. **If anxious**: Do the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise, then start with the smallest possible task.
4. **If distracted**: Move to a different location (library, quiet cafe), put your phone in another room, use the Forest app to block distractions.
5. **If the task feels too big**: Break it into micro-steps. Start with just 2 minutes on the first step.
6. **Start with a Pomodoro**: Commit to 25 minutes only — knowing there's an end makes starting easier.

### "I feel burnt out — what should I do?"

Burnout is serious — it is not cured by "pushing through." Recognise it and respond appropriately.

**Immediate steps:**
1. **Stop.** Seriously — take at least 1–2 full days off studying. Guilt-free.
2. **Sleep.** Prioritise 8–9 hours.
3. **Eat properly.** Burnout depletes your body.
4. **Move.** Even a 20-minute walk significantly reduces burnout symptoms.

**After the rest:**
5. **Talk to someone.** A trusted friend, family member, or APU counsellor.
6. **Review your load.** Are you overcommitted? Talk to your programme coordinator about extensions.
7. **Re-engage gradually.** One Pomodoro per day is enough to start. Don't try to catch up everything at once.

If burnout is severe or accompanied by persistent low mood, please contact APU Student Counselling Unit.

### "What's the best way to study for exams?"

The most evidence-backed exam preparation method:

1. **Start early** — at least 2 weeks before the exam.
2. **Past papers first** — get the past year papers and understand the exam format and common question types before revising.
3. **Active recall, not re-reading** — use flashcards (Anki), brain dumps, and practice questions. Avoid highlighting.
4. **Spaced repetition** — review material in increasing intervals (Day 1, Day 3, Day 7, Day 14).
5. **Simulate exam conditions** — time yourself on past papers without looking at notes.
6. **Prioritise weak areas** — spend more time on topics you struggle with, not the ones you already know.
7. **Sleep the night before** — memory consolidation happens during sleep. An all-nighter before an exam is counterproductive.

### "How do I stop procrastinating?"

Procrastination is avoidance, not laziness. The solution is removing the emotional barrier, not forcing willpower.

**The most effective techniques:**
- **2-Minute Start**: Commit to working for just 2 minutes. Almost always, you'll continue.
- **Identify WHY you're avoiding it**: Fear of failure? Perfectionism? Boredom? Overwhelm? The solution depends on the cause.
- **Break it down**: Replace "write essay" with "open a blank document and write one sentence."
- **Implementation intention**: "I will work on [task] at [time] at [location]." Write it down.
- **Remove the decision**: Set up your workspace the night before. Open the file before you go to sleep so it's waiting for you.

---

### "I keep re-reading my notes but nothing sticks."

Re-reading is one of the least effective study methods. It creates the "illusion of knowing" — the material feels familiar, but you haven't truly encoded it into long-term memory.

**Switch immediately to:**
- **Active recall**: Close your notes. Write down everything you remember about the topic. Check your notes for gaps.
- **Flashcards (Anki)**: Create one card per concept and review daily.
- **Practice questions**: Find past papers or create your own questions.
- **Teach it**: Explain the concept aloud as if teaching a classmate who has never seen it.

### "I have an exam tomorrow and I'm panicking."

This is common and manageable.

**Do this now:**
1. **Stop panicking about panicking** — some anxiety before exams is normal and even useful.
2. **Do 5 minutes of box breathing** to calm your nervous system.
3. **Accept what you can and can't control** — you cannot learn everything tonight. Focus on key concepts and high-frequency exam topics.
4. **Do a 1-hour targeted review**: Look at the exam format, review past papers, do active recall on the most important 3–5 topics.
5. **Stop studying by 10pm** — sleep is more valuable than cramming at 2am.
6. **Prepare practically**: Lay out your ID, stationery, water bottle tonight.
7. **Sleep** — 7–8 hours. This is the single most important thing you can do before an exam.

---

### "I have too many assignments and don't know where to start."

When overwhelmed by multiple tasks, the problem is usually lack of priority clarity, not lack of time.

**Follow this process:**
1. **Brain dump**: Write every assignment and task on one page.
2. **Add deadlines**: Next to each item, write when it's due.
3. **Priority matrix**: Categorise each as Urgent/Important using the Eisenhower Matrix.
4. **Start with the closest deadline** that is also important — not the easiest.
5. **Set a time block for each**: "Assignment A: 2 hours today. Assignment B: 1 hour tomorrow."
6. **Do the first task for just 2 minutes** to break the paralysis.

## Stress & Mental Health

### "I feel anxious all the time — is this normal?"

Some anxiety is completely normal for university students. If it is:
- Present before specific events (exams, presentations, deadlines) — this is normal performance anxiety
- Mostly manageable with breathing or planning — this is within normal range

However, if anxiety is:
- Persistent and present even when nothing specific is happening
- Preventing you from attending class, sleeping, eating, or socialising
- Coming with physical symptoms (racing heart, shortness of breath, chest pain)

...it may indicate an anxiety disorder, and you should speak to a professional. Contact APU Student Counselling Unit or a doctor.

---

### "I can't sleep before exams. What can I do?"

Pre-exam insomnia is extremely common. Here's a protocol:

**Evening before the exam:**
1. Stop studying by 9–10pm. Your brain needs time to wind down.
2. Avoid caffeine after 2pm.
3. Shower — the drop in body temperature after a warm shower promotes sleep.
4. 4-7-8 breathing: 4 in, 7 hold, 8 out — repeat 4 times.
5. Write your worries in a journal — externalising them reduces rumination.
6. Keep your room cool and dark.
7. If you can't sleep, don't panic — even lying quietly with your eyes closed is restorative.

---

### "I feel like I'm the only one struggling — everyone else seems fine."

This is a very common experience — and it's almost certainly not true.

Social media and campus culture create an illusion that everyone else is coping brilliantly. In reality:
- A 2019 study in JAMA Pediatrics found 1 in 3 university students meet criteria for anxiety, depression, or both.
- Most students feel out of depth at some point but don't talk about it.
- "Looking fine" is not the same as "being fine."

Talk to your classmates honestly — you may be surprised at how many share your experience. Seek support from APU counselling services without shame.

---

### "How do I deal with imposter syndrome?"

Imposter syndrome is the persistent feeling that you don't deserve your achievements and that you'll be "found out." It is extremely common among high achievers and does not reflect reality.

**What helps:**
1. **Write down your evidence of competence** — past assignments you passed, skills you've built, challenges you've overcome.
2. **Normalise it** — talk to classmates or a mentor. You'll find you're not alone.
3. **Separate feeling from fact** — "I feel like a fraud" is not evidence that you are one.
4. **Reframe failure** — a failed quiz is information, not identity. Everyone struggles with something.
5. **Find a mentor** — a lecturer or senior student who has navigated what you're going through.

## Habits & Lifestyle

### "How do I build a consistent study routine?"

Building a routine takes time but follows a reliable process:

1. **Start tiny** — just 1 Pomodoro per day at the same time. The consistency matters more than the duration.
2. **Same time, same place** — consistency in context helps the behaviour become automatic.
3. **Habit stack** — attach your study session to an existing habit. "After dinner, I study."
4. **Eliminate friction** — prepare your study space the night before. Have everything ready.
5. **Track it** — use a simple habit tracker (paper is fine).
6. **Never miss twice** — one missed day is fine; two in a row starts to break the habit.
7. **Reward yourself** — after each session, do something enjoyable. This reinforces the habit loop.

Expect it to feel difficult for the first 2–3 weeks. That discomfort is normal — it is the habit being established.

---

### "How do I balance studying with rest, social life, and extracurriculars?"

Balance is not about equal time — it is about intentional allocation.

**Framework:**
- Decide your non-negotiables (minimum sleep, exercise, meals)
- Schedule study blocks first around classes
- Schedule social/personal time explicitly (don't leave it to "if I have time")
- Set a study cut-off time — when you stop, you stop
- Review and adjust weekly — a Sunday planning session makes the whole week clearer

A student who works 5 focused hours per day is more effective than one who half-studies for 12 hours.

### "What should I eat to improve focus and energy?"

Nutrition significantly affects cognitive performance:

- **Breakfast matters**: Students who eat breakfast show better concentration, memory, and exam scores.
- **Avoid sugar spikes**: High-sugar foods cause energy crashes within 30–60 minutes.
- **Brain foods**: Oily fish (omega-3), nuts, berries, dark leafy vegetables, dark chocolate (in moderation), eggs.
- **Hydration**: Even mild dehydration (1–2% body weight) reduces concentration by up to 13%.
- **Caffeine**: Effective for focus in moderation (1–2 cups of coffee). Avoid after 2pm to protect sleep.
- **Eat regular meals** — skipping meals leads to brain fog and mood drops.
