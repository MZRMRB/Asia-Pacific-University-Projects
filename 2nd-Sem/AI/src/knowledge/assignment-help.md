# Assignment Help & Academic Guidance

## Getting Started When You Don't Know Where to Begin

The most common reason students struggle with assignments is not lack of ability — it is lack of clarity on how to start. This guide gives you a step-by-step process for every type of academic assignment.

---

## The Assignment Breakdown Process

### Step 1: Understand the Brief (15 minutes)

Before doing anything else, read the assignment question carefully — at least twice.

Answer these questions:
- What is being asked? (Identify the key verb: Analyse, Discuss, Compare, Evaluate, Describe)
- What is the word count and due date?
- What format is required? (Essay, report, presentation, code, case study)
- What sources are required? (Academic journals, textbooks, industry sources)
- What referencing style? (APA, Harvard, IEEE)
- What are the marking criteria / rubric? Read this carefully — it tells you exactly what the lecturer values.

**Key verbs and what they mean:**
| Verb | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Analyse | Break into components and examine each |
| Discuss | Present multiple perspectives with evidence |
| Evaluate | Make a judgement based on evidence and criteria |
| Compare | Identify similarities and differences |
| Describe | Give a clear account of what something is |
| Explain | Make something clear with reasons and examples |
| Critically assess | Question assumptions, evaluate evidence, form a position |

---

### Step 2: Research Phase (Allocate time proportional to assignment weight)

**Finding good sources:**
- **APU Library databases**: EBSCOhost, ProQuest, Google Scholar, ScienceDirect, IEEE Xplore (for IT assignments)
- **Google Scholar**: Free access to many academic papers. Use quotation marks for exact phrases.
- **Textbooks**: Chapter bibliographies often lead to excellent sources.
- Only use peer-reviewed journal articles and credible sources. Wikipedia is a starting point, not a source.

**Evaluating sources — use the CRAAP test:**
- **Currency**: When was it published? (Aim for within the last 5–10 years unless citing a foundational theory)
- **Relevance**: Does it directly address your topic?
- **Authority**: Is the author qualified? Is the journal reputable?
- **Accuracy**: Is the information evidence-based and cited?
- **Purpose**: Is it written to inform, or to sell/persuade?

**Managing sources:**
- Use a reference manager: Zotero (free) or Mendeley (free) — saves time on citations.
- As you find each source, note: Author, Year, Title, Key Argument, Page Number.
- Never copy text without immediately noting the source — you WILL forget later.

---

### Step 3: Plan Your Structure (30 minutes)

Before writing a single word of your draft, create a detailed outline.

**For Essays:**
```
INTRODUCTION (10% of word count)
- Hook / opening statement
- Background context
- Thesis statement (your main argument)
- Overview of essay structure

BODY PARAGRAPH 1 (PEEL structure)
- Point: Topic sentence stating the main idea
- Evidence: Quote or paraphrase from source
- Explanation: Analyse the evidence, link to your argument
- Link: Connect back to thesis and transition to next paragraph

BODY PARAGRAPH 2 (PEEL)
...

BODY PARAGRAPH 3+ (PEEL)
...

CONCLUSION (10% of word count)
- Restate thesis (in different words)
- Summarise key arguments
- Implications / future direction
- Closing thought
```

**For Reports:**
```
Title Page
Executive Summary / Abstract
Table of Contents
1.0 Introduction
2.0 Literature Review / Background
3.0 Methodology
4.0 Findings / Analysis
5.0 Discussion
6.0 Conclusion and Recommendations
References
Appendices (if applicable)
```

---

### Step 4: Write the First Draft

**Golden rule: Separate writing from editing.**
Your first draft is NOT the final product. Its only job is to get ideas on paper.

**Writing strategies:**
- **Start with the body, not the introduction.** The intro is easier to write after you know what you've said.
- **Write without looking at your notes first** — this helps you internalise the ideas rather than copy.
- **Don't edit while writing** — ignore grammar errors, awkward phrasing, everything. Just keep moving forward.
- **Target your Pomodoro blocks**: "In this Pomodoro, I will write the first body paragraph."
- **Leave placeholders**: If you need a citation, write [CITE] and keep going.

**If you're stuck / writer's block:**
- Set a 5-minute timer and write anything — even "I don't know how to write this section because..."
- Change your environment
- Re-read your outline and talk through what you want to say
- Write bullet points first, then convert to paragraphs

---

### Step 5: Revise and Edit (Separate sessions)

Never edit immediately after writing. Leave at least a few hours (ideally a day) between writing and editing.

**Revision process:**
1. **Big picture first**: Does the argument flow logically? Is the thesis supported throughout? Are all parts of the question answered?
2. **Paragraph level**: Does each paragraph have one clear point? Is there evidence? Is it linked back to the thesis?
3. **Sentence level**: Is every sentence clear? Are there unnecessary words?
4. **Proofreading last**: Grammar, spelling, punctuation. Read aloud — your ear catches errors your eyes miss.
5. **Word count**: Cut ruthlessly if over. Every word should earn its place.
6. **Citations**: Check every in-text citation has a corresponding reference list entry and vice versa.
7. **Formatting**: Match the assignment requirements exactly (font, spacing, margins, referencing style).

## Managing Multiple Assignments and Deadlines

### The Assignment Triage Method
When you have multiple assignments due, follow this order:

1. **List all assignments** with their due dates and percentage weights.
2. **Urgent + High weight** → Work on this first.
3. **Urgent + Low weight** → Do the minimum required to pass, then move on.
4. **Non-urgent + High weight** → Schedule dedicated time this week.
5. **Non-urgent + Low weight** → Schedule later, don't stress now.

### Backward Planning
Start from the deadline and plan backward:
- Day 0 (due): Submit
- Day -1: Final proofread and submission check
- Day -2 to -3: Full edit and revisions
- Day -4 to -6: Write remaining sections and first draft
- Day -7 to -10: Research and outline
- Day -14: Understand the brief and identify key sources

## Referencing and Academic Integrity

### Why Referencing Matters
- Referencing is not bureaucracy — it is intellectual honesty. You are crediting the ideas that built your work.
- Plagiarism (presenting others' work as your own) is academic misconduct with serious consequences at APU.
- Unintentional plagiarism (poor paraphrasing or forgotten citations) is still a violation.

### APA Referencing (7th Edition) — Common Formats

**Journal Article:**
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. *Journal Name*, *Volume*(Issue), page–page. https://doi.org/xxxxx

**Book:**
Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of work: Capital letter also for subtitle*. Publisher.

**Website:**
Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). *Title of page*. Website Name. URL

**In-text citation:**
- One author: (Smith, 2020) or Smith (2020) found that...
- Two authors: (Smith & Jones, 2020)
- Three or more: (Smith et al., 2020)
- Direct quote: (Smith, 2020, p. 45)

### Turnitin and Similarity
APU requires Turnitin submission. A similarity below 15% is generally acceptable.
- Do NOT try to trick Turnitin — it is far more sophisticated than students realise.
- Paraphrase properly: read a source, close it, write in your own words, then check and cite.
- Your reference list will show similarity but this is excluded from the score.

## Presentation and Public Speaking Tips

If your assignment includes a presentation:

### Preparation
- Know your content deeply — don't memorise a script, understand the ideas.
- Practice aloud at least 3 times, ideally in front of someone.
- Time yourself — finish 1 minute early, never go over.
- Prepare for questions: What might a lecturer ask? Practice your answers.

### Slides (if required)
- **One idea per slide**
- **Minimal text** — slides support your speaking, not replace it
- **6×6 rule**: No more than 6 bullet points per slide, 6 words per point
- Use images, diagrams, charts — visual information is processed 60,000x faster than text
- Consistent design: 2 fonts maximum, 3 colours maximum

### During the Presentation
- Make eye contact with different people, not just the lecturer
- Speak to the audience, not the screen
- Slow down — nerves make you speak faster than you realise
- Use pauses deliberately — a 2-second pause is powerful, not awkward
- If you lose your place: pause, take a breath, look at your notes calmly

### Managing Presentation Anxiety
- Box breathing before you begin (3 rounds)
- The audience wants you to succeed — they are not adversaries
- Nervous energy and excited energy feel the same in the body — reframe it as excitement
- Start with a strong opening line — confidence in the first 30 seconds sets the tone

## Getting Help from Lecturers

Many students avoid asking for help out of fear of seeming incompetent. This is counterproductive — lecturers want students to succeed.

**How to approach a lecturer effectively:**
1. Attend office hours or email to schedule a time.
2. Come prepared: "I've read the brief and I'm struggling with [specific question]. I've tried [what you've already done]. Can you help me clarify [specific thing]?"
3. Don't ask: "Can you explain the whole assignment?" — be specific.
4. If you're behind on a deadline due to genuine difficulty: communicate early. Lecturers can help with extensions if approached in advance, not at the last minute.
